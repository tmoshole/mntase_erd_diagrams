Why Mntase uses separate databases

Mntase handles different categories of sensitive information. Keeping identity, activity, reporting, and payments in separate databases limits the damage if one account or service is compromised.

The central rule: no single customer-facing system should be able to see both who a member is and their private check-ins or survey answers.
Most restricted

1. Identity Database

Answers: Who is this person, and may we contact them?

  • Encrypted names, phone numbers, and email addresses
  • Login, OTP, device session, and push-notification details
  • Full consent history and communication preferences

Access: Only the Identity Service. Other systems use a random member ID.

Pseudonymous

2. Operational Database

Answers: What happened inside the product?

  • Check-ins, survey responses, campaigns, points, and streaks
  • Mpintshi referrals and community relationships
  • Append-only events and workflow history

Protection: No names, phone numbers, emails, or payment details.

Aggregates only

3. Analytics Database

Answers: What trends can Head Office and clients safely see?

  • Ward, community, and province-level totals and trends
  • Campaign reach, response rates, and anonymised distributions
  • Mood and food-security indicators

Protection: No member IDs or raw responses. Small groups are suppressed.

Financially restricted

4. Financial Database

Answers: What must be paid or redeemed?

  • Redemption requests, payout status, and reconciliation
  • Payment-provider tokens and masked destination labels
  • Mpintshi earnings and payout batches

Protection: Store provider tokens instead of full bank or wallet details.

How the databases work together

They communicate through controlled services using random identifiers. They are not joined together directly.

1. Member joinsIdentity records contact and consent, then creates a random member ID.
2. Member participatesOperational stores the check-in or survey under that random ID.
3. Results are groupedAn analytics worker creates privacy-safe totals after checking consent.
4. Client sees resultsThe reporting API returns aggregates only, never member records.

What risk does this reduce?

  • Identity breach: Activity and financial history are not present in the stolen database.
  • Operational breach: Records contain random IDs rather than names or contact details.
  • Client-account breach: The attacker can reach only approved aggregate reports.
  • Payment breach: Check-ins, surveys, and identity records remain outside the payment database.
  • Internal mistakes: Separate credentials and service accounts reduce accidental over-access.

Recommended rollout

  1. Start with separate Identity and Operational databases.
  2. Add Analytics once check-ins and campaigns produce real data.
  3. Add Financial when real redemptions and payouts begin.
  4. Give every service its own account, permissions, backups, and audit trail.

Separation improves security, but it does not replace encryption, consent enforcement, least-privilege access, monitoring, backups, or incident procedures.
